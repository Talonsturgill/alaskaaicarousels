The homework

The business

Big Dan's Fishing Charters & Lodge is a family-owned saltwater and freshwater fishing operator on the Kenai Peninsula, run by owners Dan and Sarah Spies. Four vessels (the 50-ft Double Down and 42-ft Game Changer catamarans, plus the smaller Wanka and Blackjack) work out of Homer and Seward, guided trips run the Kenai and Kasilof Rivers, five riverfront cabins operate in Soldotna, and the business also runs vessel-based transport for spring black bear and fall Sitka blacktail deer hunts. Bookings run through FareHarbor online, or by calling Sarah directly for anything the calendar can't resolve.

The opportunity

Get a fast, reliable answer to any booking question the online calendar can't resolve, or that arrives outside business hours, without waiting on a single person's callback, across all three locations and both trip families.

This is the highest-cited, best-evidenced opportunity in the research, directly quoted on Big Dan's own site as the fallback instruction for exactly the busiest part of the business. No competitor in the Homer, Seward, or Soldotna market has moved on it yet, and accommodation and food service businesses overall show the lowest AI adoption of any sector measured, so this is a genuinely early move, not a crowded one.

What they use today

A phone call or email directly to Sarah Spies, who manually checks cross-location and cross-boat availability herself.

Why a change is realistic

Push is real, a named single point of contact across a structurally complex, three-location, two-trip-type operation during a season where demand already outstrips supply. Pull is concrete, two named small operators in adjacent trades show real gains from exactly this pattern, and it plugs into a system Big Dan's already runs. Anxiety is real too, for a five-star, review-driven brand worried about a wrong answer on a weather-sensitive booking, which is exactly why the human escalation path stays mandatory, not optional.

The recommended build

AI front-desk chat agent, FareHarbor-integrated, chat-first

Answers guest questions and books whatever FareHarbor already shows as open, 24/7, across all three locations. Anything off-calendar, cross-location, weather-sensitive, or safety-adjacent goes straight to Dan and Sarah with a clean written summary instead of a cold callback, the same handoff that happens today, just with fewer routine calls reaching them first.

Chat-first MVP. The AI only handles understanding the question and writing the reply, every availability check, booking decision, and escalation is a deterministic rule against FareHarbor and your own settings. Anything unusual goes straight to Dan and Sarah, exactly as it does now.

Where AI fits, and where it does not

The availability check and the book-or-route decision are deterministic lookups against FareHarbor, not model guesses, so the AI's job is narrowed to understanding the question and writing the reply, which keeps this at a workflow tier rather than an open-ended agent. Where we would NOT use AI, the FAQ layer over Big Dan's own three static pages (a rewritten FAQ section does that job with zero hallucination risk), the go or no-go booking decision once FareHarbor's calendar already shows a slot open or closed (a straight data read, never a model call), and any weather-related rescheduling or hunting-transport safety decision, which stays a hard rule that always escalates to Dan and Sarah.

Build vs buy

We buy the commodity underneath it, the language model, the database, email and text delivery, and telephony if the phone channel is ever added, and we build only the part that is genuinely Big Dan's, the specific booking rules, the escalation logic, and the FareHarbor wiring.

The plan

What we would build, scoped

Anyone who wants a trip or cabin not already open on the FareHarbor calendar has exactly one path forward, call Sarah, across four boats, three towns, five cabins, and two hunting seasons, through the short summer season the business tells guests fills up fast.

Goals

  • Answer availability and booking questions instantly through a chat widget, tied live to the real FareHarbor calendar.
  • Resolve the calendar's known quirk conversationally, surfacing true availability across all four boats and both trip types on request.
  • Cut routine, answerable-by-calendar phone and message traffic reaching Sarah's cell.
  • Escalate cleanly to Dan and Sarah, by name, with full context, for anything off-calendar, cross-location, weather-sensitive, or safety-adjacent.

Non-goals, on purpose

  • Phone-answering AI in Phase 1 (Telephony integration on your existing line is unconfirmed, we will not promise it before we've verified it.)
  • Autonomous booking or payment execution off the standard calendar (Unattended booking gets unreliable fast once it has to chain several judgment steps together, not a bet worth your reputation.)
  • Weather-driven rescheduling automation (High cost of error, no small operator anywhere runs this today, held out entirely.)
  • Cabin-specific guest messaging for the Soldotna cabins (No cited pain there yet, we would not invent one.)
  • A general FAQ chatbot (It would answer questions your site already answers and never touch the actual bottleneck.)

The numbers

Honest ROI, not a hero number

What it costs to run, five year view

We're deliberately not quoting a five-year commitment today. Step one, a two-to-four-week baseline check, costs no new dollars, just Sarah logging real call and contact volume before anything is built. And the decision rule is explicit, if that check shows off-calendar inquiry volume at or below the conservative assumption (roughly five a day), our honest recommendation is to stop there and not build, because the math does not clear at that volume. Step two, only if the baseline clears and FareHarbor access is confirmed, is the actual near-term ask, the chat-first walking skeleton plus a chat-only MVP for one peak season, no phone channel. That prices at roughly 8,000 to 14,000 dollars, implementation, training, one season of run cost, and contingency included. Continuing past that first measured season would add a recurring 1,500 to 2,800 dollars a year, a separate renewal decision made only after real results are in, never bundled into today's number.

The honest base rate

Most AI pilots show no real financial impact, roughly 95 percent by MIT's 2025 State of AI in Business research (source 15 below), and AI projects fail at roughly 80 percent by RAND's analysis of root causes (source 16 below). This build is designed to sit in the minority that pays, it lives inside the FareHarbor calendar already in use, it routes to the same person who already handles this today, and it's scoped to a reliability band we can stand behind, not an autonomous system making judgment calls. But that design edge doesn't exempt this case from the base rate's real lesson, most pilots fail because they're funded on assumption rather than measured baseline, which is exactly why the no-cost check comes before the dollar ask, not after. Honestly, the accommodation and tourism sector overall has the lowest AI adoption of any sector measured (source 13), so this isn't chasing a crowded trend, it's an early, deliberate move in a segment that hasn't made it yet.

Who owns the number Sarah Spies would own running the baseline check and, if it proceeds, tracking chat-handled versus escalated volume against it. Dan Spies would own the go or no-go call on the build itself, and separately on any renewal after the first season is measured.

The honest part, what could make this not work

  • We have not yet confirmed FareHarbor grants the API access this build needs, that gets checked before anything is built, not assumed.
  • The phone channel, the highest-cited pain, depends on confirming your telephony setup can support it, which is why it's a later phase, not part of the first build.
  • There is no existing baseline for how many calls or missed inquiries you actually get today, so every ROI number here is a planning assumption, not a measurement, until we run a real spot check.
  • The conservative version of the ROI case does not pay back in cash alone at any horizon we modeled, honestly. It clears faster only under more optimistic, but still realistic, assumptions, which is exactly why we'd start with a no-cost baseline check before any build dollars move.
  • The two industry examples we cite (a Florida charter fleet, a New Zealand cruise operator) are vendor-reported case studies, not independently audited, and we're not presenting them as guarantees for your business.
  • Voice response times in production systems are generally a beat slower than a person, callers notice the pause. This build doesn't include phone answering yet for exactly that reason, we'd rather confirm it works well before promising it.
