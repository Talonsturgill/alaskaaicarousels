# SELECTION + DEDUPE — Carousel No. 10 — 2026-07-18

## SELECTED STORY
**"Will a data center raise your power bill? In Alaska the honest answer splits
on one line: on the grid or off it."**

A myth-correcting ratepayer-economics analysis, pegged to two fresh in-window
decisions: the state's move to hand AIDEA about 30 square miles of free Railbelt
land near Houston for a data-center park (July 15-16), and the July 17 close of
the STAK North Slope lease comment window. The deck teaches the one distinction
that determines the answer, and names the policy gap.

The argument (all from verified claims.json):
- The fear that dominated 500-plus STAK comments (higher power bills) is
  geographically unfounded for that project: STAK is OFF-GRID, self-generating
  on North Slope gas hundreds of miles from the Railbelt, behind the meter, so
  its fuel use has no direct impact on city electricity prices (C3e, the spine,
  two independent sources).
- The project where bills genuinely could move is the ON-GRID one the state
  just advanced: AIDEA's Mat-Su park, on the Railbelt (C1, C1d).
- On the grid the answer is not one arrow. A big new "anchor" load can SPREAD
  a utility's fixed costs and lower residential rates (attributed to Launch
  Alaska's Penny Gage, C4), OR it can compete for scarce Railbelt gas and raise
  them (C4b). Which one happens is decided by the rate deal, not by physics.
- The gap: Anchorage adopted local guardrails (AO 2026-27, 10 to 2, C5b) but
  the STATE bill that would set rate-setting rules (HB 259, Rep. Mears) sits in
  the House Energy Committee (C5). No statewide cost-allocation rule exists. So
  the thing that decides your bill is a contract Alaskans have not required.

## WHY THIS STORY (criteria in order)
1. Strongest concrete Alaska impact: household electricity bills for Railbelt
   Alaskans, the kitchen-table stakes of the whole data-center wave. Both Beat A
   and Beat F independently converged on it as the "send to a coworker" story.
2. Visual potential: a single geography split (off-grid Slope vs on-grid
   Railbelt), a power bill with two divergent arrows around a pivot labeled
   "the rate deal," a scale contrast (8 vs 639 data centers), the Railbelt as a
   network with a new anchor node.
3. Tangibility: named places (Houston, Deadhorse, the Railbelt), named bodies
   (DNR, AIDEA, Anchorage Assembly, House Energy Committee), hard numbers.
4. Would an Alaskan send it to a coworker: yes. It corrects the single most
   repeated misconception in the current data-center fight and hands the reader
   the one question to ask their utility and legislator.

## DIRECTORS-ROOM SYNTHESIS (Phase 5)
Three treatments pitched (data-journalist, systems-illustrator, editorial-essayist).
All three converged on the same argument arc (split -> off-grid correction ->
on-grid project -> the two-arrow rate-deal fork -> the policy gap -> ask) and the
same intellectual heart (a fork/pivot labeled "the rate deal"). The decision was
the visual CHASSIS.

WINNER: systems-illustrator's ISOMETRIC WIRING-DIAGRAM. Reasons:
1. Artwork-craft: it is the only chassis with genuine dimensional depth (iso
   three-face light, cast shadows, atmospheric fade). The chronic scored weakness
   across recent decks is flat heroes (No.4/5/8 capped artwork-craft at 7 to 7.5).
   The flat op-ed and flat line-chart chassis would risk the same cap.
2. Story-art fusion (the biggest win): the OFF-GRID island rendered as a
   physically DETACHED slate slab with NO conduit glow makes "disconnected =
   cannot touch your bill" legible at 432px with zero words. That is a 9-to-10
   story-art-fusion move and it carries the deck's spine visually.
3. Variety: the isometric built-system hero has NEVER been the series signature
   (parked 2026-07-14 as a fresh direction). Diverges hard from every recent hero.
4. Reliability: pure Canvas-2D isometric math, no WebGL race, deterministic.

GRAFTS:
- From editorial-essayist: the sharper SYNTHESIS beat (build vs ban is the wrong
  fight; the missing option is REQUIRE THE RULES), which also resolves the systems
  treatment's self-critique (avoids two near-identical "switch" slides). Plus the
  honest attribution handling (a parchment op-ed CARD with "PER A LAUNCH ALASKA
  OP-ED" printed INSIDE the frame).
