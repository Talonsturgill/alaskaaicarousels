# Selection + Dedupe Gate — 2026-07-21 — Carousel No. 13

## DECISION: Cook Inlet beluga whales + AI acoustic monitoring

**Story.** NOAA Fisheries, Microsoft AI for Good, and Alaska Department of Fish and Game
built a deep-learning "dual-stage" framework (detection, then species classification) that
uses contrastive audio-language models and active learning to pull rare beluga calls out of
Cook Inlet's noisy, ship-heavy soundscape and strengthen daily beluga-presence estimates
(Marine Mammal Science, online June 7 2026, print July 2026, DOI 10.1111/mms.70213). A
separate NOAA-led effort (with USGS, Microsoft AI for Good, the Naval Research Laboratory)
is teaching a cloud model to spot the same whales in very-high-resolution satellite imagery.
The stakes: the Cook Inlet beluga is a genetically distinct, geographically isolated,
ESA-endangered (2008) population that fell nearly 80 percent from about 1,300 (1979) to 279
(2018); the best current estimate is 331, which NOAA says may be stabilizing and possibly
increasing, yet the population "has not recovered as expected" even with the subsistence hunt
suspended since 2007 and two critical-habitat areas designated in 2011.

**Thesis (honesty spine).** A better ear is not a recovery. AI is giving Alaska's most
endangered whale a sharper, cheaper, always-on set of ears (and eyes), which tells us WHERE
and WHEN the belugas are in a wall of ship noise, but hearing is not healing: the machine
listens, the population's problems (noise, prey, a crowded inlet) are still there, and 331 is
cautious hope, not a rescue. The honest, analytical Alaska.Ai position: measure first, then
decide, and do not mistake a better instrument for a recovered whale.
